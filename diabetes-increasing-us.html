Why Is Diabetes Increasing? Alarming Statistics, Common Causes, Surprising Risk Factors, and Myths Debunked

Little Wins Health · Updated July 2026 · 18 min read · Educational content only — always talk to your doctor about your own health.

Diabetes is increasing worldwide, and the latest diabetes statistics show that the number of people living with the condition continues to rise. Health experts are seeing more people diagnosed with diabetes than ever before, making it one of the fastest-growing chronic health conditions globally.

While rising obesity rates and physical inactivity remain major drivers, researchers are also exploring other factors that may be contributing to this trend.

Understanding why diabetes is increasing is the first step toward protecting your health. In this guide, you'll discover the latest diabetes statistics, the most common and surprising diabetes risk factors, who is most at risk, and practical ways to help prevent type 2 diabetes.

We'll also debunk common diabetes myths and explain what the latest research really says, so you can make informed decisions with confidence.

🌿 Little Wins tip

Diabetes statistics tell an important story, but they don't predict your future. Small, consistent habits, such as eating a balanced diet, staying active, getting enough sleep, and keeping up with regular health checkups, can support your long-term health and help lower your risk of type 2 diabetes.

Is Diabetes Increasing? The Latest Statistics

Quick answer

Yes. Diabetes is increasing worldwide. The latest diabetes statistics show that the number of people living with diabetes has risen dramatically over the past three decades. The increase is being driven by a combination of factors, including rising obesity rates, aging populations, physical inactivity, unhealthy dietary patterns, and other emerging risk factors discussed later in this guide.

At a glance:

  • Diabetes cases have increased from 200 million in 1990 to 830 million in 2022 worldwide.
  • Approximately 589 million adults were living with diabetes in 2024.
  • Experts project that number will reach 853 million adults by 2050 if current trends continue.
  • More than 40 million Americans have diabetes.
  • An estimated 115 million U.S. adults have prediabetes.

Diabetes is no longer considered a disease affecting only a small portion of the population. Today, it is one of the fastest-growing chronic health conditions worldwide, placing an increasing burden on individuals, healthcare systems, and national economies.

According to the World Health Organization (WHO), the number of people living with diabetes increased from 200 million in 1990 to 830 million in 2022, more than a fourfold increase in just over three decades. This rapid rise reflects changing lifestyles, longer life expectancy, urbanization, and other factors that increase the risk of developing diabetes.

The International Diabetes Federation (IDF) estimates that approximately 589 million adults aged 20 to 79 were living with diabetes in 2024, representing 1 in every 9 adults worldwide. If current trends continue, the organization projects that the number of adults living with diabetes will increase to 853 million by 2050.

You may notice that the WHO and IDF report different totals. This is expected because the organizations use different methodologies and populations. The WHO reports broader global estimates, while the IDF focuses specifically on adults aged 20 to 79 years. Despite these differences, both organizations show the same clear trend: diabetes continues to increase worldwide.

The United States reflects the same pattern. According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), more than 40 million Americans are living with diabetes, and 90% to 95% of those cases are type 2 diabetes. The CDC also estimates that approximately 115 million U.S. adults have prediabetes, a condition that significantly increases the risk of developing type 2 diabetes if left untreated.

These statistics show that millions of people are already living with elevated blood sugar levels without experiencing obvious symptoms. As a result, many individuals are not diagnosed until complications begin to develop, highlighting the importance of regular health screenings and early intervention.

Together, these figures paint a clear picture. Diabetes is increasing across every region of the world, affecting younger and older adults alike. Understanding why these numbers continue to rise is the first step toward reducing your own risk and making informed decisions about your long-term health.

What Are the Common Causes of Diabetes?

Quick answer

There isn't a single cause of diabetes. The condition develops through a combination of genetic, lifestyle, and environmental factors. While the exact causes vary depending on the type of diabetes, type 2 diabetes, which accounts for 90% to 95% of all diabetes cases, is most commonly linked to insulin resistance, excess body weight, physical inactivity, unhealthy dietary patterns, family history, aging, and certain medical conditions.

The most common risk factors for type 2 diabetes include:

  • Insulin resistance
  • Excess body weight, particularly around the abdomen
  • Physical inactivity
  • Unhealthy dietary patterns
  • Family history of diabetes
  • Age
  • A history of gestational diabetes
  • Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)

Although diabetes is often discussed as a single disease, it actually refers to a group of conditions that affect how the body regulates blood sugar (glucose). Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition in which the immune system mistakenly attacks the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas. Type 2 diabetes, the most common form, develops gradually when the body becomes resistant to insulin or can no longer produce enough insulin to keep blood sugar levels within a healthy range.

Because type 2 diabetes accounts for the vast majority of cases, most research on diabetes prevention focuses on the factors that contribute to insulin resistance and long-term metabolic health.

Insulin Resistance

Evidence strength: Strong

Insulin is a hormone produced by the pancreas that helps move glucose from your bloodstream into your body's cells, where it is used for energy. When your cells stop responding to insulin as effectively as they should, the pancreas produces more insulin to compensate. Over time, this extra workload can strain the pancreas, allowing blood sugar levels to rise.

Insulin resistance often develops gradually over many years, which is why people can have prediabetes without experiencing noticeable symptoms. If insulin resistance continues to worsen, it can eventually progress to type 2 diabetes.

Excess Body Weight

Evidence strength: Strong

Carrying excess body weight, particularly around the abdomen, is one of the strongest risk factors for developing type 2 diabetes. Abdominal fat, also known as visceral fat, is metabolically active and releases substances that can interfere with how the body responds to insulin.

It's important to remember that body weight is only one piece of the puzzle. Some people living with obesity never develop diabetes, while others at a healthy weight can develop the condition because of genetics, visceral fat, or other underlying risk factors.

Physical Inactivity

Evidence strength: Strong

Regular physical activity helps your muscles use glucose more efficiently and improves insulin sensitivity. Spending long periods sitting or engaging in very little physical activity can reduce the body's ability to regulate blood sugar over time.

You don't need intense workouts to support your health. Walking, cycling, swimming, gardening, and other moderate activities performed consistently can all help improve insulin sensitivity and reduce the risk of type 2 diabetes.

Unhealthy Dietary Patterns

Evidence strength: Strong

A dietary pattern that regularly includes large amounts of ultra-processed foods, refined carbohydrates, sugary drinks, and foods high in saturated fats may contribute to weight gain, insulin resistance, and poor blood sugar control over time.

On the other hand, eating a balanced diet built around the best foods for managing blood sugar, such as vegetables, fruits, whole grains, legumes, lean proteins, nuts, and healthy fats, can support healthy blood sugar regulation and overall metabolic health.

Family History

Evidence strength: Strong

Your genes can influence how your body produces and uses insulin, meaning diabetes often runs in families. Having a parent or sibling with type 2 diabetes increases your risk, but it does not guarantee that you will develop the condition.

Lifestyle choices remain important, even for people with a strong family history. Regular physical activity, balanced eating habits, maintaining a healthy weight, and routine health screenings can help lower the risk or delay the onset of type 2 diabetes.

Age

Evidence strength: Strong

Although type 2 diabetes can develop at any age, the risk generally increases as people get older. This is partly because aging is often associated with reduced muscle mass, lower physical activity levels, and a greater likelihood of developing other health conditions that affect blood sugar regulation.

However, increasing numbers of children, teenagers, and young adults are now being diagnosed with type 2 diabetes, highlighting that age is no longer the only factor to consider.

Gestational Diabetes

Evidence strength: Strong

Gestational diabetes develops during pregnancy when the body cannot produce enough insulin to meet the increased demands of pregnancy. Blood sugar levels usually return to normal after giving birth, but women who have had gestational diabetes have a higher risk of developing type 2 diabetes later in life.

Regular follow-up care and healthy lifestyle habits after pregnancy can help reduce that long-term risk.

Polycystic Ovary Syndrome (PCOS)

Evidence strength: Strong

Women with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS) are more likely to develop insulin resistance, making them more susceptible to prediabetes and type 2 diabetes. While not everyone with PCOS will develop diabetes, healthcare providers often recommend regular blood sugar screening as part of ongoing care.

Managing body weight, staying physically active, and following a balanced eating pattern can help improve insulin sensitivity and lower the risk of diabetes in women with PCOS.

🌿 Little Wins tip

You can't change your age or your genes, but many diabetes risk factors are manageable. Small lifestyle changes, such as moving your body more, eating balanced meals, maintaining a healthy weight, and attending regular health checkups, can have a meaningful impact on your long-term health.

What Are the Surprising Risk Factors for Diabetes?

Quick answer

While excess body weight, physical inactivity, and family history remain the most well-established risk factors for type 2 diabetes, researchers have identified several other factors that may also influence diabetes risk. These include long-term exposure to air pollution, endocrine-disrupting chemicals, poor sleep, changes in the gut microbiome, certain medications, excess visceral fat in people of any body size, and chronic inflammation. Although the strength of the evidence varies, these findings highlight that diabetes is influenced by more than diet and exercise alone.

Scientists now understand that diabetes develops through a complex interaction of genetics, lifestyle, environment, and metabolism. Some of these factors are well established, while others are still being investigated. Learning about these emerging risk factors can help you better understand your overall health without causing unnecessary worry. Many are beyond your control, but healthy lifestyle habits can still make a meaningful difference.

Endocrine-Disrupting Chemicals (EDCs)

Evidence strength: Emerging

Endocrine-disrupting chemicals are substances that can interfere with the body's hormone system. They are found in everyday items such as some plastic containers, food packaging, pesticides, cosmetics, and household products.

Researchers are studying whether long-term exposure to certain EDCs may increase the risk of insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes by disrupting hormones involved in blood sugar regulation and metabolism. While the evidence continues to grow, scientists are still working to understand how much exposure is needed to affect health and which chemicals pose the greatest risk.

Simple steps, such as avoiding heating food in plastic containers, choosing glass or stainless steel when possible, and washing fruits and vegetables before eating them, may help reduce unnecessary exposure.

Air Pollution

Evidence strength: Moderate

Air pollution is most commonly associated with lung and heart disease, but growing evidence suggests it may also contribute to diabetes.

Tiny airborne particles, particularly fine particulate matter (PM2.5), can trigger inflammation and oxidative stress throughout the body. Over time, these changes may reduce insulin sensitivity and increase the risk of developing type 2 diabetes.

You don't need to avoid going outdoors altogether. On days with poor air quality, limiting prolonged outdoor exercise, using indoor air filtration if available, and checking local air quality reports may help reduce exposure.

Gut Microbiome Imbalances

Evidence strength: Moderate

Your digestive system is home to trillions of bacteria that help digest food, produce vitamins, support the immune system, and influence metabolism. Together, these microorganisms are known as the gut microbiome.

Researchers have found that people with type 2 diabetes often have different gut bacteria compared with those without diabetes. Although scientists are still determining whether these changes are a cause or a consequence of diabetes, maintaining a healthy gut microbiome through a balanced diet rich in fibre, fruits, vegetables, legumes, and fermented foods may support overall metabolic health.

TOFI: Thin Outside, Fat Inside

Evidence strength: Strong

Not everyone who develops type 2 diabetes is overweight or obese. Some people appear to have a healthy body weight but carry excess visceral fat, the fat stored around internal organs.

This pattern is sometimes referred to as TOFI, or Thin Outside, Fat Inside. Visceral fat is more closely linked to insulin resistance than the fat stored just beneath the skin. As a result, people with a normal body weight can still have an increased risk of diabetes if they carry high amounts of visceral fat.

This is one reason healthcare providers consider more than body weight when assessing diabetes risk.

Poor Sleep

Evidence strength: Strong

Sleep plays an important role in regulating hormones that influence appetite, insulin sensitivity, and blood sugar levels.

Consistently getting too little sleep or experiencing poor-quality sleep has been linked to an increased risk of insulin resistance and type 2 diabetes. Conditions such as obstructive sleep apnoea can further increase that risk by repeatedly disrupting normal sleep patterns.

Most adults should aim for 7 to 9 hours of quality sleep each night as part of an overall healthy lifestyle.

Artificial Sweeteners

Evidence strength: Emerging

Artificial sweeteners are often marketed as a healthier alternative to sugar because they contain few or no calories.

Some studies suggest that certain sweeteners may influence the gut microbiome or glucose metabolism, while others have found little or no effect. Because research findings remain mixed, experts have not concluded that artificial sweeteners directly cause diabetes.

If you use sugar substitutes, moderation remains a sensible approach. Replacing sugary drinks with water is still one of the most effective ways to reduce added sugar intake.

Certain Medications

Evidence strength: Strong

Some medications can increase blood sugar levels or reduce insulin sensitivity in certain individuals. Examples include some corticosteroids, certain antipsychotic medications, and a few medicines used to treat autoimmune conditions.

This does not mean people should stop taking prescribed medication without medical advice. The benefits of treatment often outweigh the potential risks, and healthcare providers can monitor blood sugar levels when necessary.

If you're concerned about how a medication may affect your diabetes risk, speak with your healthcare provider before making any changes.

Research Spotlight: The SCAN Enzyme

Evidence strength: Emerging

Recent research has identified an enzyme known as SCAN as a potential contributor to insulin resistance. Early laboratory studies suggest that blocking this enzyme may improve the body's response to insulin.

While these findings are promising, they are still in the early stages of research. More clinical studies involving people are needed before this discovery can influence diabetes prevention or treatment.

For now, SCAN represents an exciting area of scientific investigation rather than an established cause of diabetes.

🌿 Little Wins tip

Not every risk factor is within your control, and that's okay. Focus on the habits you can influence, such as eating a balanced diet, staying physically active, getting enough sleep, attending regular health checkups, and avoiding tobacco. Small, consistent choices often have a greater impact on long-term health than striving for perfection.

Who Is Most at Risk of Developing Diabetes?

Quick answer

Anyone can develop diabetes, but some people have a higher risk than others. Your risk depends on a combination of factors, including your age, family history, body composition, medical history, ethnicity, and lifestyle habits. Having one or more risk factors doesn't mean you'll definitely develop diabetes, but it does mean you may benefit from regular health screenings and healthy lifestyle choices.

While diabetes can affect people of all ages and body types, certain factors make it more likely to develop, particularly type 2 diabetes. Some of these, such as your family history or age, can't be changed. Others, including physical activity, diet, and weight management, are areas where healthy habits can help reduce your risk.

According to the National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases (NIDDK), you may have a higher risk of developing type 2 diabetes if you:

  • Are 45 years or older. The risk of type 2 diabetes increases with age, although diagnoses among younger adults and children have also become more common.
  • Have prediabetes. Blood sugar levels that are higher than normal, but not yet high enough for a diabetes diagnosis, significantly increase the likelihood of developing type 2 diabetes.
  • Have overweight or obesity. Carrying excess body weight, especially around the abdomen, can contribute to insulin resistance.
  • Have a parent or sibling with diabetes. A family history of diabetes increases your risk because both genetic and shared lifestyle factors can play a role.
  • Had gestational diabetes during pregnancy. Women who develop diabetes while pregnant have a greater chance of developing type 2 diabetes later in life.
  • Have polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS). PCOS is commonly associated with insulin resistance, increasing the risk of prediabetes and type 2 diabetes.
  • Have high blood pressure or unhealthy cholesterol levels. These conditions are often linked with metabolic syndrome, which increases the risk of diabetes and cardiovascular disease.
  • Live a mostly sedentary lifestyle. Spending long periods sitting and getting little regular physical activity can reduce insulin sensitivity over time.
  • Belong to certain ethnic groups. Some populations have a higher risk of developing type 2 diabetes, including people of African, Hispanic or Latino, Native American, Alaska Native, Asian American, Pacific Islander, and South Asian backgrounds. Researchers believe this increased risk results from a combination of genetic, environmental, socioeconomic, and lifestyle factors rather than ethnicity alone.
  • Have a history of heart disease or stroke. These conditions often share many of the same underlying risk factors as type 2 diabetes, including insulin resistance, obesity, high blood pressure, and unhealthy cholesterol levels.

It's important to remember that risk factors are not guarantees. Many people with multiple risk factors never develop diabetes, while others with few obvious risk factors receive a diagnosis. That's why regular health checkups and screening tests are so important, especially if you have concerns about your personal risk.

🌿 Little Wins tip

Knowing your risk is empowering, not discouraging. If you have one or more risk factors, talk with your healthcare provider about when to have your blood sugar checked. Early detection can help you make changes before diabetes develops or identify the condition early, when it's often easier to manage.

Common Diabetes Myths Debunked

Quick answer

Diabetes is surrounded by misconceptions that can make it difficult to separate fact from fiction. Some myths discourage people from getting tested, while others create unnecessary fear about food, weight, or treatment. Understanding what the science actually says can help you make informed decisions about your health.

Myth #1: Eating Sugar Causes Diabetes

✓ FactSugar alone does not cause diabetes.

Type 2 diabetes develops because of a combination of factors, including genetics, insulin resistance, excess body weight, physical inactivity, and long-term dietary patterns. While regularly consuming foods and drinks high in added sugars can contribute to weight gain and increase the risk of insulin resistance, enjoying sugar occasionally is not the direct cause of diabetes. A balanced eating pattern matters much more than any single food.

Myth #2: Only People with Obesity Develop Type 2 Diabetes

✓ FactPeople of any body size can develop type 2 diabetes.

Although overweight and obesity are significant risk factors, they are not the only ones. Genetics, age, family history, visceral fat, PCOS, gestational diabetes, and certain medications can all increase diabetes risk. Some people with a healthy body weight also develop type 2 diabetes, while many people living with obesity never develop the condition.

Myth #3: People with Diabetes Can't Eat Fruit

✓ FactFruit can be part of a healthy diet for people with diabetes.

Whole fruits contain natural sugars, but they also provide fibre, vitamins, minerals, and antioxidants. Fibre helps slow the absorption of sugar into the bloodstream, making whole fruit a healthier choice than sugary snacks or sweetened drinks.

Portion sizes and the overall eating pattern remain important, so it's best to choose whole fruits rather than fruit juices whenever possible.

Myth #4: Type 2 Diabetes Only Affects Older Adults

✓ FactPeople of all ages can develop type 2 diabetes.

Although the risk increases with age, healthcare providers are diagnosing type 2 diabetes more often in younger adults, teenagers, and even children. Rising rates of childhood obesity, reduced physical activity, and dietary changes have all contributed to this trend.

Myth #5: If You Have Prediabetes, You'll Definitely Develop Diabetes

✓ FactPrediabetes does not always progress to type 2 diabetes.

Prediabetes is a warning sign, not a guarantee. Many people are able to delay or prevent type 2 diabetes by becoming more physically active, eating a balanced diet, maintaining a healthy weight, and following their healthcare provider's advice. Early action can make a significant difference.

Myth #6: Diabetes Is Contagious

✓ FactYou cannot catch diabetes from another person.

Diabetes is not caused by a virus or bacteria, and it cannot be spread through touching, sharing food, kissing, coughing, or any other form of casual contact. Although researchers are still studying what triggers type 1 diabetes in some people, neither type 1 nor type 2 diabetes is contagious.

This misconception may seem surprising, but surveys have shown that some people still mistakenly believe diabetes can spread from person to person.

Myth #7: Insulin Means You've Failed

✓ FactNeeding insulin is not a sign of failure.

Some people with diabetes can manage their condition through lifestyle changes and oral medications, while others eventually need insulin because their pancreas no longer produces enough of the hormone. This is a natural progression for many people and does not mean they did something wrong.

The goal of diabetes treatment is to keep blood sugar within a healthy range and reduce the risk of complications, regardless of which treatment plan is needed.

Myth #8: You Can Always Tell If Someone Has Diabetes

✓ FactMany people with diabetes have no obvious symptoms.

Type 2 diabetes often develops gradually, and some people have no noticeable symptoms for years. Others may experience increased thirst, frequent urination, unexplained weight loss, blurred vision, fatigue, or slow-healing wounds.

Because symptoms may be mild or absent, regular health screenings are especially important for people with risk factors.

Myth #9: People with Diabetes Can't Eat Sugar Ever

✓ FactPeople with diabetes don't have to eliminate sugar completely from their diet.

A diabetes diagnosis doesn't mean you can never enjoy a slice of birthday cake or your favorite dessert again. What matters most is your overall eating pattern, the amount of carbohydrates you consume, and portion sizes, rather than avoiding sugar entirely.

Many people with diabetes can include small amounts of foods containing added sugar as part of a balanced meal plan, especially when blood sugar is well managed. Choosing nutrient-rich foods most of the time while enjoying treats occasionally is often a more realistic and sustainable approach than trying to avoid sugar completely.

Your healthcare provider or a registered dietitian can help you develop a meal plan that fits your individual health needs and blood sugar goals.

🌿 Little Wins tip

Question health advice that sounds too simple. Diabetes is a complex condition, and many popular claims shared online are incomplete or inaccurate. When in doubt, look for information from trusted health organizations and discuss any concerns with your healthcare provider.

Frequently Asked Questions About Diabetes

Can diabetes be cured?

At present, there is no cure for diabetes. However, many people with type 2 diabetes can achieve remission by making significant lifestyle changes, such as losing weight if recommended by their healthcare provider, becoming more physically active, and following a balanced eating pattern. Remission means blood sugar levels remain below the diabetes range without diabetes medication for an extended period, but ongoing monitoring is still important because diabetes can return.

Type 1 diabetes currently has no cure and requires lifelong insulin therapy.

What are the early warning signs of diabetes?

Many people with type 2 diabetes develop symptoms gradually, while others have no noticeable symptoms at all. Common early signs include:

  • Increased thirst
  • Frequent urination
  • Feeling unusually tired
  • Blurred vision
  • Slow-healing cuts or wounds
  • Unexplained weight loss
  • Increased hunger
  • Frequent infections

If you notice these symptoms or have several risk factors for diabetes, speak with your healthcare provider about having your blood sugar tested.

Can type 2 diabetes be prevented?

Although type 2 diabetes cannot always be prevented, healthy lifestyle habits can significantly reduce your risk or delay its onset.

Regular physical activity, eating a balanced diet, maintaining a healthy weight, getting enough sleep, avoiding tobacco, and attending routine health screenings all support healthy blood sugar regulation. These habits are particularly important for people with prediabetes or a family history of diabetes.

What's the difference between type 1 and type 2 diabetes?

Type 1 diabetes is an autoimmune condition in which the immune system attacks the insulin-producing cells in the pancreas. People with type 1 diabetes produce little or no insulin and require lifelong insulin treatment.

Type 2 diabetes develops when the body becomes resistant to insulin or can no longer produce enough insulin to keep blood sugar levels within a healthy range. It is far more common than type 1 diabetes and is influenced by a combination of genetic, lifestyle, and environmental factors. For a fuller side-by-side comparison, see our guide to type 1 vs. type 2 diabetes.

How often should I get tested for diabetes?

The answer depends on your age, personal health, and risk factors.

Many health organizations recommend regular diabetes screening for adults beginning around age 35, particularly if they have overweight or obesity. Earlier or more frequent testing may be recommended if you have prediabetes, a family history of diabetes, had gestational diabetes, have PCOS, or belong to a higher-risk group.

Your healthcare provider can recommend the most appropriate screening schedule for your individual circumstances.

Can children develop type 2 diabetes?

Yes. Although type 2 diabetes was once considered a condition that primarily affected adults, it is now being diagnosed more frequently in children and teenagers.

Researchers believe this increase is linked to several factors, including rising childhood obesity rates, reduced physical activity, dietary changes, family history, and socioeconomic influences. Early diagnosis and treatment are important to help reduce the risk of long-term complications.

When should I see a healthcare provider?

You should consider speaking with a healthcare provider if you:

  • Have symptoms of diabetes
  • Have prediabetes
  • Have a family history of diabetes
  • Had gestational diabetes during pregnancy
  • Have concerns about your blood sugar
  • Have several diabetes risk factors

Seeking medical advice early can lead to earlier diagnosis, timely treatment, and a lower risk of complications.

Final Thoughts

Diabetes is becoming more common around the world, but a diagnosis, a family history, or a risk factor is never the whole story. Some things you can't change, and that's okay. What you can do is start where you are, with what you have. You don't need to overhaul your life overnight, and you certainly don't need to be perfect.

This is the heart of Little Wins Health: real progress is built from small, doable steps, not dramatic ones. Choosing a balanced meal. Going for a short walk after dinner. Getting a proper night's sleep. Booking that checkup you've been putting off. Learning one new thing about your body, like you just did.

None of those feel like much on their own. But little wins add up. Repeated gently, day after day, they grow into habits that quietly protect your health for years to come. So be kind to yourself, celebrate the small stuff, and take the next step whenever you're ready. You've got this. 💚
