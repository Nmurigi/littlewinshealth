Is Diabetes Increasing in the US? What the Newest Data Shows — and What It Means If You've Just Been Diagnosed

Little Wins Health · Updated July 2026 · 6 min read · Educational content only — always talk to your doctor about your own health.

Yes — diabetes is increasing in the US. In 2024, 12.7% of American adults reported having diabetes, up from 11.2% in 2020. That's roughly a 12% rise in just five years, according to CDC survey data compiled by PopHIVE at the Yale School of Public Health.

If you or someone you love was just diagnosed, that number means something important: you are far from alone. About 1 in 8 adults in this country is living with the same condition — going to work, raising kids, cooking dinner, and figuring it out one day at a time. This article walks through what the data shows, and then gets to what matters most: what to do next.

What the numbers show

Here's the picture in plain terms:

  • 12.7% of US adults had diabetes in 2024 — up from 11.2% in 2020 (CDC BRFSS data via PopHIVE, Yale School of Public Health)
  • The rise has been steady, year after year — this isn't a blip
  • Among Medicare beneficiaries (mostly people 65 and older), about 26% have diabetes — so if you're older, the odds that people around you understand what you're going through are even higher

Why is diabetes rising?

There's no single villain. A few things are happening at once:

  • We're living longer. Type 2 diabetes becomes more common with age, so an aging country means more cases.
  • Weight and inactivity. More of us carry extra weight and move less than our bodies were built for — the two biggest drivers of type 2 diabetes.
  • We're also finding it more. Better screening means more people learn they have diabetes instead of living with it undiagnosed. That part is actually good news — you can't manage what you don't know about.

None of this is about blame. Diabetes has genetic, environmental, and economic roots. A diagnosis says nothing about your character.

Where you live matters

Diabetes doesn't hit every place equally. The highest rates in 2024 were well above the national median of 12.4% (CDC BRFSS via PopHIVE):

Why does this matter to you? Because where you live shapes access to fresh food, safe places to walk, and specialists. If you're in a high-rate area, your community likely has more support programs than you'd expect — diabetes education classes, community health workers, and support groups. Ask your doctor's office what exists locally; you may be surprised.

When to get screened

If you're reading this because someone you love was diagnosed, you might be wondering about yourself. Good instinct — type 2 diabetes runs in families.

The American Diabetes Association recommends screening for all adults starting at age 35, and earlier if you're overweight with other risk factors like family history or high blood pressure. Screening is a simple blood test (often an A1C test) at a routine visit.

One number worth knowing: tens of millions of Americans have prediabetes — blood sugar that's elevated but not yet diabetes — and most don't know it. Caught early, prediabetes can often be turned around. If it's been a while since your last check-up, this is your nudge.

You've just been diagnosed. Now what?

Take a breath. The weeks after a diagnosis can feel like drinking from a firehose — new words, new numbers, new rules. You do not have to change everything today. Here's a calm starting point:

Ask your doctor these four questions:

  1. What's my A1C now, and what are we aiming for?
  2. Do I need medication, or are we starting with lifestyle changes?
  3. How often should I check my blood sugar, if at all?
  4. Can you refer me to a diabetes educator or nutrition counselor? (Many insurance plans cover this — it's one of the most underused benefits.)

Then pick ONE habit to start with — not five:

  • Try the plate method. No counting, no math: fill half your plate with non-starchy vegetables, a quarter with lean protein, a quarter with carbs. That's it.
  • Or take a 10-minute walk after your biggest meal. Moving after eating helps your muscles use the sugar from your food.

That's the whole philosophy here: you don't have to be perfect. You get better every day when you do one small thing — and small things add up to real numbers on your next lab test.

What others living with diabetes are doing

You're joining a community of millions, and it helps to know what's working for them:

  • Peer support. Research on diabetes online communities has found that people who take part in them score higher on self-care measures than those who don't. Whether it's a Facebook group, a subreddit, or an in-person class, hearing "me too" from someone a year ahead of you is powerful.
  • Simple tracking. Many people keep a low-effort log — blood sugar readings, meals, walks — not to judge themselves, but to spot patterns to discuss with their doctor.
  • Meal-prep routines. Cooking a few diabetes-friendly staples on Sunday removes daily decision fatigue, which is half the battle.
  • Bringing family in. People do better when the household eats the same healthy meals rather than the person with diabetes eating "special food" alone. If you're the loved one reading this: joining in is one of the kindest things you can do.
